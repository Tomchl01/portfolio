The collusion problem in poker

Poker is built on incomplete information. You can see your own cards and the shared cards on the table, but not what your opponents are holding. That hidden information is the entire game. Collusion happens when two or more players at the same table secretly share that information with each other, or coordinate their decisions to move money in a direction that benefits their group. It is cheating, and it is surprisingly hard to prove.

What makes it difficult is not that the behavior is exotic. It is that the behavior is ordinary. Players fold, call, and bet all day long. Most of the time those decisions are completely legitimate. The question is how you tell the difference when the data looks the same either way.

01 · The Problem

The problem with looking at individuals

If you pull up a player's stats and they look normal, you move on. That is the natural instinct. Win rate is fine, fold frequency looks right, nothing jumps out. The problem is that collusion does not always show up in individual profiles at all. Two players can look completely unremarkable on their own while quietly and consistently benefiting each other across hundreds of hands.

The anomaly lives in the relationship between them, not in either player alone.

That's what makes it genuinely hard to catch. Most fraud leaves a footprint — unusual login locations, a transaction that doesn't fit the account's history, a withdrawal that trips a rule. In poker, the colluding player is doing legal things: calling, folding, betting. The only thing wrong is the reason behind those decisions, and that reason doesn't appear in any per-player report.

Volume is the first wall

A moderately busy online poker room generates tens of thousands of hand histories a day. Two to nine players per hand, multiple decisions per player per street — aggregate that over a month and you're in the millions of individual records. Nobody's reading that manually.

The hard part isn't finding something suspicious — it's knowing which suspicious things are actually worth pursuing. Cast the net too wide and you drown in variance. Too narrow and real cases stay hidden. The data has to do the filtering first, or nothing scales.

02 · Why It's Hard

Why simple signals fail

The first thing most people look at is how often two players share a table. They keep sitting together — that must mean something. It usually doesn't. Players have preferences: specific stakes, specific hours, specific game types. In a smaller pool you naturally run into the same faces over and over. Co-play frequency alone is noise.

Chip flow is more useful, but it still falls apart on its own. Poker variance is real — you can run badly against one person for weeks through pure bad luck. You need enough hands before any concentration in flow starts to mean something, and even then it's not proof. It's a reason to look harder, nothing more.

No single signal is enough. Each question just narrows the field. Something only moves up the list when several independent angles land on the same pair, for different reasons, at the same time.

03 · The Approach

You need more than one signal

The unit of suspicion isn't a player — it's a pair. Two people whose history, laid out side by side, looks wrong in ways that neither of them would look wrong alone. Getting there means cutting through a lot of ordinary data, and the only way to do it reliably is to ask several independent questions and only care when more than one comes back hot.

Three questions do the work. Where is the money going? Does this player act differently when that specific person is at the table? Do the decisions make sense, or do they quietly go against the player's own interest?

None of them settles anything alone. Together, they're hard to explain away.

Layer one: follow the chips

The most concrete thing you can measure in poker is chip flow. At the end of every hand, money moves from some players to others. Over thousands of hands, you can map exactly how much each player won or lost against every specific opponent they faced.

Healthy players have diffuse chip flow — win some here, lose some there. What stands out is concentration: one player sending a disproportionate share of their losses consistently to the same destination. That alone doesn't prove anything, but it's a meaningful filter. At this volume, any good filter matters.

What matters is whether that concentration is unusual given how many opponents they faced and how many hands they actually shared. You have to normalize for opportunity — otherwise you're just flagging pairs who sat together a lot, which doesn't tell you anything.

Layer two: behavior changes at the table

Once chip flow looks unusual, the question becomes whether the player's decisions actually change when that specific person sits down.

Take a specific situation type — a particular position, stack depth, facing a particular bet size — and compare how a player acts in that spot across all opponents versus against one specific person. If the numbers shift meaningfully, that's worth attention.

A player might call bets aggressively everywhere, then fold far more often in identical spots when one specific person is betting. Or stop raising their strong hands when that person is in the pot. Neither thing is impossible by chance. But when the gap is large and holds across hundreds of hands, chance becomes a hard argument to make.

Layer three: are the decisions defensible?

The third layer is the most precise. Solver software can calculate what a rational player should do in any given spot, and serious players tend to cluster close to those benchmarks in aggregate. Nobody plays perfectly — but the distribution of their mistakes has a shape.

When someone's decisions against one specific opponent consistently deviate from what the math would suggest — and that deviation keeps costing them chips that land in the same spot — the picture gets hard to dismiss. Chip flow concentrated on one edge. Behavior that shifts around one person. Decisions that quietly work against their own interest. Three separate observations. One pattern.

04 · The Signals

Seeing it in the data

You can describe a pattern in words. Seeing it is different. The visualizations below are synthetic — no real players, no real hands — but the structures are real. This is what each signal actually looks like.

Why network methods work

Model a poker table as a directed graph: each player is a node, every chip transfer at the end of a hand becomes a weighted, directed edge. Run that over thousands of hands and you have a complete record of who sent value where and how much — something individual stat-sheets can't give you.

  • Edge weight concentration: in a fair game, losses distribute roughly evenly across opponents. Unusual volume concentrated on one edge is a signal that the relationship between those two nodes is not random.
  • Local pair structure: repeated patterns between a specific pair matter more than global table volume. Two players can look normal in aggregate while being anomalous relative to each other.
  • What graphs capture that stats hide: individual metrics flatten relationships into per-player averages. Graph representation preserves direction, pairing, and concentration. Those are exactly the dimensions where collusion lives.

Everyone plays suboptimally sometimes. Variance, pressure, fatigue — it adds up, and that's expected. What isn't expected is when the worst decisions cluster around one specific opponent, and all the value that bleeds away keeps landing in the same spot. At some point, variance isn't a satisfying answer.

05 · Reflection

What it does well

Each signal works independently — chip flow doesn't know what the behavioral analysis found, and GTO deviation doesn't know what the network showed. When all three land on the same pair anyway, it's hard to write off as coincidence. That's what makes the results worth acting on.

Individual player stats flatten everything into per-person averages and miss the point entirely. A graph preserves direction, pairing, and concentration — exactly the dimensions where collusion actually hides.

Where it falls short

These signals prioritize cases — they don't prove anything. Every flagged pair still needs a human to look at it. The method narrows the field; it doesn't close the case. I think that distinction matters a lot, and any real deployment should make it explicit.

A few things it doesn't catch well:

  • Short-session colluders. Pairs who keep their cooperation to brief, high-stakes bursts can stay under the statistical threshold. Sample size is a hard requirement.
  • Rotating partners. If colluders switch who they're helping each session, the flow patterns get diffuse enough to look normal on any individual edge.
  • Genuine bad luck against one opponent. Variance is real. You can run badly against the same person for weeks through pure misfortune. The method reduces false positives but can't eliminate them.
  • Three-way or larger groups. The pair-based structure misses organized rings of three or more. Detecting those requires community detection rather than edge concentration — a different problem entirely.

What this is an example of

Poker works as a test case because the rules are precise and every decision gets logged. But the same problem shows up across fraud, AML, and network security: actors who look normal on their own and only reveal themselves through their relationships. The pair-first structure and the layered-signal approach aren't poker-specific. They apply anywhere the unit of wrongdoing is a relationship rather than an individual.
