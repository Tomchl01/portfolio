Detecting Collusion in No-Limit Hold'em

Hand histories are noisy. Hundreds of thousands of decisions flow through an online poker room every day, and most of them are entirely ordinary. The challenge of detecting collusion is not finding suspicious hands — it is finding the ones that matter, building a case that holds up to scrutiny, and doing it at scale without burying investigators in false positives. This is a behind-the-scenes account of how I approached that problem.

The problem with looking at individuals

If you pull up a player's stats and they look normal, you move on. That is the natural instinct. Win rate is fine, fold frequency looks right, nothing jumps out. The problem is that collusion does not always show up in individual profiles at all. Two players can look completely unremarkable on their own while quietly and consistently benefiting each other across hundreds of hands.

The anomaly lives in the relationship between them, not in either player alone.

This makes poker collusion genuinely hard to catch. In most fraud problems, the bad actor does something that sticks out: unusual login patterns, a transaction that does not fit the account history, a withdrawal that breaks a rule. In poker, the colluding player is allowed to call, fold, or bet. Those are legal actions. The only thing that makes them wrong is the reason behind them, and that reason is invisible to any system that looks at one player at a time.

Volume is the first wall

The second challenge is scale. A moderately busy online poker room generates tens of thousands of hand histories a day. Each hand can have anywhere from two to nine players, each making three to fifteen decisions across multiple streets. By the time you aggregate that into something a human investigator can actually review, you are looking at millions of individual decision records per month. No team is reading all of that.

So anything you build has to triage. It needs to surface the cases that deserve a human's attention and leave behind the ones that do not. Get the triage wrong in one direction and investigators drown in noise. Get it wrong in the other direction and real colluders go undetected. Both outcomes are failures, just different kinds.

Single signals break down fast

The naive starting point is a heuristic: flag any pair of players who share a high percentage of hands together. That sounds reasonable until you remember that players have table preferences, play on the same stakes for years, and some smaller player pools naturally overlap constantly. Co-play frequency tells you almost nothing on its own.

You could try looking at chip flow instead. Flag pairs where one player keeps losing chips to the same opponent. Closer, but still fragile. Poker has genuine variance. A player can run bad against one specific person for a long stretch just by chance. You need a lot of hands before chip flow concentration becomes statistically meaningful, and even then it is not proof of anything. It is just a reason to look closer.

This is where I landed on the core design principle for this system: no single signal is enough. The job is to stack independent signals so that each one narrows the search, and a case only escalates when multiple layers agree.
