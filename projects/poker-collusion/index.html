The collusion problem in poker

Poker is built on incomplete information. You can see your own cards and the shared cards on the table, but not what your opponents are holding. That hidden information is the entire game. Collusion happens when two or more players at the same table secretly share that information with each other, or coordinate their decisions to move money in a direction that benefits their group. It is cheating, and it is surprisingly hard to prove.

What makes it difficult is not that the behavior is exotic. It is that the behavior is ordinary. Players fold, call, and bet all day long. Most of the time those decisions are completely legitimate. The question is how you tell the difference when the data looks the same either way.

The problem with looking at individuals

If you pull up a player's stats and they look normal, you move on. That is the natural instinct. Win rate is fine, fold frequency looks right, nothing jumps out. The problem is that collusion does not always show up in individual profiles at all. Two players can look completely unremarkable on their own while quietly and consistently benefiting each other across hundreds of hands.

The anomaly lives in the relationship between them, not in either player alone.

This makes poker collusion genuinely hard to catch. In most fraud problems, the bad actor does something that sticks out: unusual login patterns, a transaction that does not fit the account history, a withdrawal that breaks a rule. In poker, the colluding player is allowed to call, fold, or bet. Those are legal actions. The only thing that makes them wrong is the reason behind them, and that reason is invisible to any system that looks at one player at a time.

Volume is the first wall

The second challenge is scale. A moderately busy online poker room generates tens of thousands of hand histories a day. Each hand can have anywhere from two to nine players, each making three to fifteen decisions across multiple streets. By the time you aggregate that into something a human investigator can actually review, you are looking at millions of individual decision records per month. No team is reading all of that.

So anything you build has to triage. It needs to surface the cases that deserve a human's attention and leave behind the ones that do not. Get the triage wrong in one direction and investigators drown in noise. Get it wrong in the other direction and real colluders go undetected. Both outcomes are failures, just different kinds.

Why simple signals fail

The first instinct most people have is to look at how often two players share a table. If they keep showing up together, maybe that means something. It rarely does. Players develop preferences for certain stake levels, certain time zones, certain game types. In smaller player pools it is entirely normal to sit across from the same faces day after day. Frequency of co-play is noise, not signal.

Chip flow is more interesting, but it still breaks down in isolation. Poker has genuine variance. A player can lose money consistently to one specific opponent for weeks just by running into bad luck at the wrong moments. That happens. You need a large enough sample before any concentration in chip flow starts to mean something, and even then it is not proof of anything. It just tells you there is something worth looking at more carefully.

The core principle that holds the whole thing together is simple: no single signal is enough. Each layer of the analysis narrows the field. A case only becomes a case when several independent tests point at the same pair, for different reasons, at the same time.

You need more than one signal

The unit of suspicion in collusion is not a player. It is a pair. Two people whose history, when you lay it out side by side, looks wrong in ways that neither of them would look wrong on their own. Getting to that pair means cutting through an enormous amount of ordinary data, and the only way to do that reliably is to ask several independent questions and only pay attention when more than one of them comes back with a concerning answer.

Three questions turned out to be the right structure. The first: where is the money going? The second: does this player behave differently when that specific person is at the table? The third: do the decisions themselves make sense, or do they consistently go against the player's own interest?

None of those questions settles anything alone. Together, they are quite hard to explain away.

Layer one: follow the chips

The most concrete thing you can measure in poker is chip flow. At the end of every hand, money moves from some players to others. Over thousands of hands, you can map exactly how much each player won or lost against every specific opponent they faced.

Healthy players have chip flow that is spread around. They win some hands against this person, lose some against that one, and over time the picture looks roughly diffuse. What you are looking for is concentration: a pair where one player is sending a disproportionate amount of their losses consistently to the same destination. That is the first gate. It does not prove anything. But it narrows the field significantly, which is exactly what you need at this stage.

The important word is concentration. It is not just about total volume of chips moving between two players. A player who loses a lot to one opponent might just be running bad or playing weaker opponents. What matters is whether that concentration is statistically unusual given how many opponents they faced and how many hands they shared. You have to normalize for opportunity, otherwise you are just flagging players who sat at the same table a lot.

Layer two: behavior changes at the table

If the chip-flow gate fires, the next question is whether the player's behavior actually shifts when the suspect partner is involved. This is the behavioral fingerprinting layer, and it is where the analysis gets more interesting.

The idea is to take a specific type of situation, a particular position at the table, a particular stack depth, a particular betting pattern facing them, and compare how one player acts in that situation against all their opponents versus how they act in the same situation against one specific opponent. If the numbers are meaningfully different, that is a sign worth paying attention to.

For example: a player might call bets aggressively across the board, but suddenly become passive and fold far more often in identical spots when one particular player is betting into them. Or they might stop raising their strong hands when that player is in the pot. Neither of those things is impossible by coincidence. But when the gap is large and consistent across hundreds of hands, coincidence starts to look like a poor explanation.

Layer three: are the decisions defensible?

The third and most technically demanding layer asks whether the decisions themselves make sense from a game theory perspective. Modern poker has a well-developed body of research around optimal play. Solver software can calculate, for a given situation, what a rational and unexploitable player should do. Not everyone plays perfectly, but professionals and serious players tend to cluster around those benchmarks in aggregate.

When a player's decisions in contested spots against one specific opponent consistently deviate from what optimal play would suggest, and that deviation consistently costs themselves chips while benefiting that opponent, you have the third signal. Not a proof. But at this point you have a pair who triggered chip-flow concentration, show behavioral differences against each other, and whose decisions go against their own interests in ways that pattern toward one person. That combination is worth putting in front of a human investigator.
